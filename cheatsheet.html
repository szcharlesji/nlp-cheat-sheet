NLP Midterm Cheat Sheet — CS2590 (Lectures 1–7) — Side A

L1: TEXT CLASSIFICATION

NLP Challenges

Ambiguity: syntactic ("I saw a man with a telescope") & semantic (polysemy/background (education/real)). Situated understanding: meaning depends on social/cultural context. Data sparsity: long tail of language, rare words, low-resource langs. Bias: models amplify biases from training data; lack common sense.

Penn Treebank: parse trees are broken into grammar rules such as S→NP VP, NP→DT NN, VP→Vt NP / VP PP, PP→IN NP, which are used to estimate PCFG rule probabilities from annotated data.

Example: “The man sleeps” breaks as S→NP VP, NP→DT NN (“The man”), VP→Vi (“sleeps”); tree probability is the product of the rule probabilities.

History

1940s–50s: Shannon's info theory, per-word log-likelihood, Shannon Game. 1950s–80s: Chomsky, rule-based ("Colorless green ideas sleep furiously" — grammaticality ≠ probability). 1990s: statistical NLP, Penn Treebank, PCFGs. 2010s+: deep learning, large-scale neural models.

Classification Setup

Input $x$ (text), output $y \in C$ (finite label set), classifier $f(x)=y$. Binary (2 classes) vs. multiclass (>2). Supervised learning: train/dev/test splits. Dev for hyperparameter tuning, test for final eval. Sensitive to distribution shift.

Evaluation Metrics

$$\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP+FP} \quad \text{Recall} = \frac{TP}{TP+FN} \quad F_1 = \frac{2PR}{P+R}$$

Accuracy = correct/total — misleading w/ class imbalance (e.g., 99% non-spam → always predict non-spam = 99% acc but 0% recall on spam). F1 is harmonic mean of P and R.

Feature Representation

Bag-of-Words (BoW): $\phi(x) \in \mathbb{R}^{|V|}$, each dim = word count or binary. Ignores word order & semantic similarity. Longer docs have larger feature vectors → more influence on learning.

Logistic Regression

Binary (sigmoid):

$$P(y\!=\!1 \mid x,w) = \sigma(w \cdot \phi(x)) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-w \cdot \phi(x)}}$$

Multiclass (softmax):

$$P(y \mid x,w) = \frac{e^{w_y \cdot \phi(x)}}{\sum_{y'} e^{w_{y'} \cdot \phi(x)}} \quad \hat{y} = \arg\max_y w_y \cdot \phi(x)$$

Loss: NLL (cross-entropy): $\mathcal{L}(w) = -\sum_i \log P(y_i \mid x_i,w)$. NLL is convex → local min = global min. SGD: $w \leftarrow w - \eta \nabla_w \mathcal{L}$. L2 reg: add $\lambda\|w\|^2$ → penalizes large weights → prevents overfitting.

Perceptron

Predict: $\hat{y} = \text{sign}(w \cdot \phi(x))$. Update: only on mistakes: $w \leftarrow w \pm \phi(x)$ (add for false neg, subtract for false pos). Convergence: guaranteed if linearly separable. Has mistake bound. vs. LR: no probabilistic output; updates only on errors; LR updates on every example.

Feedforward Neural Networks

$$P(y \mid x) = \text{softmax}(W \cdot g(V \cdot \phi(x) + b_1) + b_2)$$

Activations: Sigmoid $\sigma(z)=\frac{1}{1+e^{-z}}$, Tanh, ReLU $\max(0,z)$. Key: FFNN w/o non-linearity = single linear transform = logistic regression. XOR: not linearly separable; NN with hidden layer solves it. Backprop: chain rule for efficient gradient computation through layers.

Regularization Techniques

L2 reg: penalize large weights via $\lambda\|w\|^2$. Dropout: during training, randomly zero out neurons with probability $p$. Forces redundant reps, reduces co-adaptation. At test time, scale by $(1-p)$ or use inverted dropout.

L2: WORD EMBEDDINGS

Motivation & Distributional Semantics

One-hot: $\mathbb{R}^{|V|}$, sparse, no similarity ($w_i^T w_j = 0$ for $i \neq j$). Distributional hypothesis: "know a word by the company it keeps" (Firth). Goal: dense, low-dim vectors $\mathbb{R}^d$ ($d \ll |V|$). Brown clusters: hierarchical agglomerative clustering by co-occurrence; hard clustering.

Word2Vec — CBOW & Skip-Gram

CBOW: predict center word from context. Skip-gram: predict context from center word.

$$P(c \mid w) = \frac{e^{v_c^T v_w}}{\sum_{c'} e^{v_{c'}^T v_w}}$$

Two vector sets: word embeddings $v_w$ and context embeddings $v_c$. Total params: $2 \times |V| \times d$.

Training Efficiency

Hierarchical softmax: binary tree over vocab → $O(\log|V|)$ vs $O(|V|)$. Negative sampling: for each positive $(w,c)$, sample $k$ negative pairs. Binary classification:

$$\mathcal{L} = \log \sigma(v_c^T v_w) + \sum_{i=1}^{k} \log \sigma(-v_{c_i}^T v_w)$$

Even if two words always co-occur, they may be sampled as negatives (depends on context window).

GloVe (Pennington 2014)

Global co-occurrence matrix $X$. Weighted least squares:

$$\mathcal{L} = \sum_{i,j} f(X_{ij})(w_i^T \tilde{w}_j + b_i + \tilde{b}_j - \log X_{ij})^2$$

$f(x)$ caps very frequent pairs (e.g., $f(x) = \min((x/x_{\max})^\alpha, 1)$).

fastText (Bojanowski 2017)

Skip-gram on character n-grams. Word vec = sum of n-gram vecs. Key: generates embeddings for OOV words by composing known n-grams.

Evaluation & Bias

Intrinsic: word similarity (cosine sim), analogies ($\vec{king} - \vec{man} + \vec{woman} \approx \vec{queen}$). Extrinsic: downstream task performance. Bias: embeddings capture societal biases (man→doctor, woman→nurse). TF-IDF: sparse, high-dim; $\text{tf-idf}(t,d) = \text{tf}(t,d) \times \text{idf}(t)$.

DAN, ELMo, Contextualized Embeddings

DAN: avg word embeddings → FFN → classify. Simple, loses order. ELMo: contextualized from bidirectional LSTM — same word gets different vectors in different contexts (polysemy). Embeddings = function of entire input sentence.

L3: SEQUENCE MODELING

Sequence Labeling Tasks

POS tagging: assign grammatical category (noun, verb, adj, etc.) to each word. Same word can have multiple tags by context ("back" = adj/noun/adv/verb). NER: identify & classify named entities. BIO scheme: B-TYPE (beginning), I-TYPE (inside), O (outside).

Language Modeling

Assign probability to word sequences. Chain rule:

$$P(x_1,\ldots,x_n) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} P(x_i \mid x_1,\ldots,x_{i-1})$$

Noisy Channel Model

$$\hat{x} = \arg\max_x P(x \mid a) = \arg\max_x \underbrace{P(a \mid x)}_{\text{channel}} \cdot \underbrace{P(x)}_{\text{LM}}$$

Apps: ASR ($a$=audio, $x$=text), MT ($a$=source, $x$=target), grammar correction. $P(a \mid x)$ = acoustic/translation model, $P(x)$ = language model prior.

N-gram Language Models

Markov: $P(x_i \mid x_1,\ldots,x_{i-1}) \approx P(x_i \mid x_{i-k+1},\ldots,x_{i-1})$. Estimate by counting n-gram occurrences. Memory $\propto V^N$ (exponential). Add-one (Laplace) smoothing: add 1 to all counts — overestimates rare events. Perplexity:

$$\text{PP}(x) = 2^{-\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} \log_2 P(x_i \mid x_{\lt i})}$$

Lower = better. Only comparable between models with same vocabulary.

Intuition: perplexity = average branching factor: how many next-token choices the model is effectively unsure among. Lower = less surprised.

Neural Language Models

Feedforward neural LM: concat embeddings of prev $n$ words → hidden layers → softmax over $|V|$. Limits: fixed context size; no weight sharing across positions. DAN: avg embeddings → FFN; loses word order entirely.

Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs)

$$h_t = g(Wh_{t-1} + Ux_t + b) \qquad y_t = \text{softmax}(Vh_t + b_o)$$

$W,U,b$ shared across all timesteps. Modes: Transducer — output per step (POS, LM). Acceptor — single output from final $h_n$ (classification). Limits: (1) sequential, no parallelism; (2) vanishing/exploding gradients → hard to learn long-range deps.

LSTM (Long Short-Term Memory)

Uses gates (input $i_t$, forget $f_t$, output $o_t$) with sigmoid to control info flow. Cell state $c_t$ carries long-range info:

$$f_t = \sigma(W_f[h_{t-1},x_t]+b_f) \quad i_t = \sigma(W_i[h_{t-1},x_t]+b_i)$$ $$o_t = \sigma(W_o[h_{t-1},x_t]+b_o) \quad \tilde{c}_t = \tanh(W_c[h_{t-1},x_t]+b_c)$$ $$c_t = f_t \odot c_{t-1} + i_t \odot \tilde{c}_t \quad h_t = o_t \odot \tanh(c_t)$$

Mitigates vanishing gradients. Other solutions: GRU (simpler gating), attention, gradient clipping, skip connections.

Seq2Seq Models

Encoder: RNN → final hidden state = context vector $c$. Decoder: RNN initialized with $c$ → generates output tokens autoregressively. Teacher forcing: feed ground-truth prev token during training (stabilizes). Scheduled sampling: gradually transition from ground-truth to model's own predictions (bridges train-test gap).

Exposure bias: train sees gold previous tokens; test sees the model's own outputs, so mistakes can compound.

Attention Mechanism

Motivation: fixed-size context vector is bottleneck for long sequences. Decoder attends to ALL encoder hidden states at each step.

$$e_{ij} = f(h_i^{\text{dec}}, h_j^{\text{enc}}) \quad \alpha_{ij} = \frac{e^{e_{ij}}}{\sum_k e^{e_{ik}}} \quad c_i = \sum_j \alpha_{ij} h_j^{\text{enc}}$$ $$P(y_i) = \text{softmax}(W[c_i; h_i^{\text{dec}}])$$
Score FunctionFormula
Additive (Bahdanau)$v^T \tanh(W_1 h_i + W_2 h_j)$
Dot-product (Luong)$h_i^T h_j$
Bilinear (Luong)$h_i^T W h_j$

Additive: most expressive (learnable non-linear combination); slower. Dot-product: fastest, simplest; assumes q,k same dim. Bilinear: intermediate — adds a learned interaction matrix.

L3/L4: TRANSFORMER

Advantages over RNNs

(1) Parallelism: all tokens processed simultaneously. (2) O(1) path length between any two positions (vs. O(n) for RNNs) → easier to learn long-range deps.

Self-Attention (Scaled Dot-Product)

$$Q = XW^Q, \quad K = XW^K, \quad V = XW^V$$ $$\text{Attn}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\!V$$

Intuition: like a fuzzy hash table — each query matches every key to varying degrees, then retrieves a weighted blend of values. Lets every token look at every other token for context.

Scaling by $\sqrt{d_k}$ prevents large dot products → softmax saturation → vanishingly small gradients. For large $d_k$, dot products grow $\propto d_k$ in variance.

Multi-Head Attention

$$\text{MH}(Q,K,V) = \text{Concat}(\text{head}_1,\ldots,\text{head}_h)W^O$$ $$\text{head}_i = \text{Attn}(QW_i^Q, KW_i^K, VW_i^V)$$

Each head has separate $W_i^Q, W_i^K, W_i^V$ matrices. Allows attending to different representation subspaces. Bug: if same Q,K for all heads → identical attention maps → defeats purpose.

Intuition: each head tracks a different relationship type (e.g., subject, modifier, coreference). Splitting into heads avoids one softmax having to express a single dominant pattern.

Positional Embeddings

Transformer has no recurrence → no inherent sense of order. Add positional info to input embeddings:

$$PE_{(pos,2i)} = \sin\!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d}}\right) \quad PE_{(pos,2i+1)} = \cos\!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d}}\right)$$

RoPE: rotates Q,K vectors by position angle. Used in modern LLMs (LLaMA, etc.). More effective than additive positional embeddings for extrapolation.

Sinusoidal: fixed, deterministic, generalizes to unseen lengths in theory. RoPE: encodes relative position via Q,K rotation; extrapolates better than absolute embeddings; applied in each attention layer, not just input.

Encoder Layer

Self-Attn → Add & LayerNorm → FFN → Add & LayerNorm. FFN:

$$\text{FFN}(x) = \max(0, xW_1 + b_1)W_2 + b_2 \;\;\text{(two linear layers + ReLU)}$$

Residual connections: $\text{out} = \text{LayerNorm}(x + \text{Sublayer}(x))$. Helps gradient flow in deep networks. LayerNorm: normalizes to zero mean, unit variance per layer → stabilizes training.

Decoder Layer

Three sub-layers: (1) Masked self-attention — prevents attending to future positions (causal mask: set future entries to $-\infty$ before softmax). Ensures prediction at position $i$ depends only on positions $< i$. (2) Encoder-decoder attention — Q from decoder, K and V from encoder output. (3) FFN. Each with residual + LayerNorm.

Decoder-Only Architecture (GPT-style)

Stack of masked self-attention layers only (no encoder). Autoregressive: generates one token at a time. Final layer: linear projection + softmax over vocabulary. Used by GPT-2/3, LLaMA.

Efficiency Concerns / Modifications to Attention

Self-attn complexity: $O(n^2 \cdot d)$ where $n$ = seq length. where $d$ = dimension length. KV cache: during inference, cache K,V for all past tokens → avoid recomputation. Grouped Query Attention (GQA): share K,V caches across attention heads; each group of query heads shares one K,V (intermediate between multi-head and multi-query). Multi-Head Latent Attention: map input to smaller latent vectors $c_t^Q$, $c_t^{KV}$ before mapping to K,V,Q; compresses to bottleneck first → less memory.

L4/L5: TOKENIZATION & DECODING

Tokenization

Pre-tokenization: regex-based splitting by spaces/punctuation (prevents cross-word merges like "ing to"). BPE (Byte-Pair Encoding): (1) Init vocab with all bytes/chars. (2) Count adjacent pair frequencies. (3) Merge most frequent pair → new token. (4) Repeat until desired vocab size. Open vocabulary: handles OOV via subword decomposition. Shares representations between related words ("love", "loving"). Unigram LM tokenization: start with large vocab, iteratively prune tokens with lowest likelihood impact. Encoding: text → token IDs. Decoding: token IDs → text.

BPE vs Unigram: BPE greedily builds vocabulary by merges; Unigram starts large, then deletes unlikely pieces. Constructive vs. pruning.

Decoding Strategies

Autoregressive generation: generate one token at a time conditioned on all previous. Complexity: $O(k \cdot p \cdot L + k^2 \cdot L)$ for $p$ prompt tokens, $k$ generated tokens, $L$ layers.

MethodHow It WorksPros/Cons
Greedy$\hat{x}_t = \arg\max_{x_t} P(x_t \mid x_{<t})$Fast; locally optimal ≠ globally optimal

Temperature $T$: $P'(x) \propto P(x)^{1/T}$. $T > 1$ → flatter (more random). $T < 1$ → sharper (more deterministic). $T = 1$ → original distribution. $T \to 0$ → greedy. $T \to \infty$ → uniform.Top-K vs Top-P: Top-K uses a fixed number of candidates; Top-P uses a variable set covering enough probability mass, so it adapts to sharp vs. flat distributions.

Degeneration

Beam search can yield repetitive, generic text because models are locally normalized: each next token can be likely while the full sequence is bad. Analogy: all-heads is the most likely coin-flip sequence, but atypical. Motivates sampling.

Advanced Decoding

Constrained decoding: structural (valid JSON, grammar) or lexical (force/ban words). Contrastive decoding: score $= \log P_{\text{large}}(x) - \log P_{\text{small}}(x)$ → fluent and specific (amplifies what the large model knows that the small model does not). DEXPERTS: expert + anti-expert mixture for controlled generation (steers generation toward the expert's style and away from the anti-expert's). Test-time scaling: generate $N$ candidates → verifier scores → select best (max) or weighted vote. Speculative decoding: small draft model proposes $k$ tokens → large model verifies in one parallel pass (amortizes cost: one cheap sequential draft + one parallel verify pass). Speeds up inference without changing output distribution.

Hardware-Aware Optimizations

FlashAttention: fuses attention computation, reduces HBM↔SRAM memory transfers. Exact attention (not approximate). PagedAttention: manages KV cache in non-contiguous memory pages (like OS virtual memory) → reduces memory waste.

Intuition: FlashAttention avoids materializing huge attention matrices in memory; PagedAttention stores KV cache in chunks so memory packs more efficiently.

L6: PRE-TRAINING

LM Architecture Taxonomy

Intuition: encoder-only = understand, decoder-only = generate, encoder-decoder = read then write.

BERT

Encoder-only. Base: 12 layers, 768 hidden, 12 heads, 110M. Large: 24 layers, 1024 hidden, 16 heads, 340M. Training objectives: (1) MLM: mask 15% of tokens (80% [MASK], 10% random, 10% unchanged) → predict original. (2) NSP: given sentence A and B, predict if B is the actual next sentence. Usage: [CLS] token for sentence-level classification; token outputs for NER; predict start/end positions for extractive QA.

BERT Extensions

RoBERTa: more data, longer training, dynamic masking (re-mask each epoch), removes NSP, larger batches. DeBERTa: disentangled attention (separate content and position), enhanced mask decoder.

Encoder-Decoder Models

T5: text-to-text: every task is input string → output string; task prefix specifies what to do. Trained on C4 (Colossal Cleaned Common Crawl) with span corruption denoising. BART: denoising autoencoder — corrupt text (mask, delete, permute, rotate) → reconstruct. MASS: mask contiguous span in encoder → predict in decoder.

Paradigms for Using LMs

Pre-train + Fine-tune: pre-train on massive unlabeled corpus → fine-tune on task-specific labeled data. ELMo: contextualized embeddings from biLSTM (feature-based, not fine-tuned). Prompting / In-context Learning (ICL): frozen model + prompt with instructions ± few-shot examples. Prompt Learning: template $f_{\text{template}}: x \to x'$ with slot [Z]; $\hat{z} = \arg\max_z P(f_{\text{fill}}(x',z); M_\theta)$ (e.g. sentiment: "[X]. Overall, it was a [Z] movie"). Instruction tuning: fine-tune on diverse tasks formatted as instructions. ICL is sensitive to (1) example choice, (2) ordering, (3) format/template. LLaMA 2: SFT on smaller high-quality data. Issues: data quality, hallucinations, lack of creativity.

Building LMs — Key Stages

(1) Tokenizer building (BPE). (2) Pre-training (next-token prediction). (3) Instruction tuning (SFT). (4) RLHF alignment. Training data principles: scale + quality + diversity. Curation: dedup, safety filter, cleaning, heuristic filtering. Synthetic data increasingly used. Notable datasets: The Pile (825 GB), FineWeb (108 TB), Dolma (3T tokens).

Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT)

Prefix tuning: learnable continuous prefix vectors are injected at every transformer layer, while prompt tuning adds soft prompt tokens only at the input layer. BitFit: fine-tune only bias terms (tiny fraction of params). Adapters: insert small FFN layers into each transformer layer; train only adapters. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation):

$$W' = W + \Delta W = W + BA \;\;\text{where}\;\; B \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times r}, A \in \mathbb{R}^{r \times d}, \; r \ll d$$

Only $B$ and $A$ are trained. Drastically reduces trainable parameters (e.g., $r = 8$ vs. $d = 4096$).

Intuition: freeze the big matrix and learn only a small low-rank correction, so adaptation is cheap.

L7: POST-TRAINING & RLHF

Goals of Post-Training

Align model to human preferences (helpful, harmless, honest). Distill reasoning ability. Extend context length. Post-training turns a base LM into a useful assistant.

RL Formalization & LLM Mapping

MDP comps: $S$ states, $s_1 \in S$ initial state, $A$ actions, $T:S \times A \to S$ transition, $r:S \times A \to \mathbb{R}$ reward. Policy: $\pi(a \mid s)=\mathbb{P}(A=a \mid S=s)$ = distribution over actions given state.

$$\tau=(a_1,s_1,\ldots,a_T,s_T) \qquad \max_\theta \; \mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim p_\theta(\tau)}\left[\sum_{t=1}^T r(s_t,a_t)\right]$$

LLM mapping: $s_t=(x_1,\ldots,x_{t-1})$, $a_t=x_t$, $T(s,a)=[s;a]$, policy $=p_\theta(x_t \mid x_{<t})$, trajectory = full generation $x_1,\ldots,x_T$. RL = choose token distribution so sampled completions get high total reward.

REINFORCE (Policy Gradient)

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta)=\mathbb{E}_{\tau \sim p_\theta(\tau)}\left[\left(\sum_{t=1}^T \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t \mid s_t)\right)\left(\sum_{t=1}^T r(s_t,a_t)\right)\right]$$

Key idea: log-derivative trick turns gradient of expectation into expectation of gradient. Monte Carlo: sample $N$ trajectories, form $\hat{g}$, update $\theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha \hat{g}$. LLM intuition: sample $N$ generations, score each, multiply every token log-prob gradient by total reward; positive reward pushes sampled tokens up, negative reward pushes them down. Issues: high variance; credit assignment = late/sparse reward gives weak signal about which token mattered.

Reward Model Training

Why pairwise? Humans are noisy at absolute 1-10 scoring, more consistent at "which is better?". Data: $(x,y_w,y_l)$ = prompt, winning output, losing output.

$$P(y_w \succ y_l)=\sigma\big(r_\theta([\bar{x};\bar{y}_w]) - r_\theta([\bar{x};\bar{y}_l])\big)$$ $$\mathcal{L}_r=-\mathbb{E}\left[\log \sigma\big(r_\theta([\bar{x};\bar{y}_w]) - r_\theta([\bar{x};\bar{y}_l])\big)\right]$$

Bradley-Terry: reward difference induces preference probability. Intuition: binary ranking loss raises winner score, lowers loser score. Iterative: e.g. Llama 2 used multiple rounds: train model/ranker -> sample -> annotate -> retrain; more data + bigger models usually improve RM quality.

RLHF Pipeline

RL objective: learn a policy $\pi_\theta$ that maximizes the expected return, i.e., the expected sum of rewards over a trajectory sampled from the policy.

REINFORCE algorithm: 1) sample trajectories $\tau^1,\dots,\tau^N$ from $\pi_\theta$; 2) compute gradient estimate using total reward of each trajectory; 3) update policy by gradient ascent $\theta \leftarrow \theta + \alpha \nabla_\theta J(\theta)$; 4) repeat until convergence.

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta)\approx \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\sum_{t=1}^{T}\nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t^i\mid s_t^i)\right)\left(\sum_{t=1}^{T} r(s_t^i,a_t^i)\right)$$

Step 1 — Preference data: for each prompt, generate two outputs; human picks preferred one → dataset of $(x, y_w, y_l)$ triplets. Pairwise comparison more reliable than absolute ratings.

Step 2 — Reward model: fit pairwise ranker $r_\theta$. Step 3 — optimize policy: either PPO (explicit RM + RL) or DPO (direct preference loss, no separate RM).

PPO-based RLHF (4 models): uses a policy to generate responses, a reward model to score them, a value model to estimate advantages, and a frozen reference policy to apply a KL penalty and keep the policy close to the SFT model.

PPO steps: 1) sample prompts and generate responses from the current policy; 2) score each response with the reward model and add KL penalty against the reference policy; 3) use the value model to compute advantages; 4) update the policy with the PPO objective and update the value model, then repeat.

$$\max_\theta \; \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\pi_\theta(a\mid s)}{\pi_{\theta_{\mathrm{old}}}(a\mid s)}\hat{A}(s,a) - \beta\,\mathrm{KL}\!\left(\pi_{\mathrm{ref}}(\cdot\mid s),\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s)\right)\right]$$

PPO

$$\arg\max_\theta \; E_\pi\left[\frac{\pi_\theta(a\mid s)}{\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(a\mid s)} \hat{A}(s,a) - \beta\, \mathrm{KL}[\pi_{\theta_{\text{old}}}(\cdot\mid s),\pi_\theta(\cdot\mid s)]\right]$$

Advantage: $A_\pi(s,a)=r(s,a)+V_\pi(T(s,a)) - V_\pi(s)$ = how much better action was than expected. Critic: learned value $V_\phi(s)$ estimates expected future reward, giving a baseline and lowering variance vs raw reward. KL term: keeps policy near previous/reference policy for stability - rubber band to SFT model.

4 models in memory: policy $\pi_\theta$, value network $V_\phi$, reward model $r$, reference policy $\pi_{\text{ref}}$ (frozen SFT copy). On-policy: must sample fresh completions from current policy each iteration. Intuition: PPO = REINFORCE + critic baseline + trust-region/KL control; more hyperparams/engineering, but more stable.

DPO (2 models): uses only a trainable policy and a frozen reference policy; it does not require a separate reward model or value model.

DPO steps: 1) collect preference pairs $(x,y_w,y_l)$ with winner and loser responses; 2) compute policy and reference log-probabilities for both responses; 3) optimize a preference loss that increases the winner’s relative probability and decreases the loser’s; 4) repeat over the dataset.

DPO (Direct Preference Optimization)

Motivation: PPO/RLHF is heavy: reward model training, RL instability, 4 models in memory. Can preferences be learned directly? Starting point: same KL-constrained RL objective. Key insight: solve for optimal policy, plug into Bradley-Terry; partition function $Z(\bar{x})$ cancels.
$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{DPO}}(\theta) = -E_{(\bar{x},\bar{y}_w,\bar{y}_l) \sim \mathcal{D}}\left[\log \sigma\left(\beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(\bar{y}_w\mid\bar{x})}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(\bar{y}_w\mid\bar{x})} - \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(\bar{y}_l\mid\bar{x})}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(\bar{y}_l\mid\bar{x})}\right)\right]$$

Implicit reward: $\hat{r}(\bar{x},\bar{y}) = \beta \log \frac{\pi_\theta(\bar{y}\mid\bar{x})}{\pi_{\text{ref}}(\bar{y}\mid\bar{x})}$, so no separate reward model. 2 models only: trainable policy $\pi_\theta$ + frozen reference $\pi_{\text{ref}}$. Off-policy: trains on fixed preference dataset, no fresh rollout loop.

$$\nabla \mathcal{L} = -\beta E\left[\underbrace{\sigma(\hat{r}_l - \hat{r}_w)}_{\text{higher when wrong}}\left(\underbrace{\nabla \log \pi_\theta(\bar{y}_w\mid\bar{x})}_{\uparrow \text{preferred}} - \underbrace{\nabla \log \pi_\theta(\bar{y}_l\mid\bar{x})}_{\downarrow \text{dispreferred}}\right)\right]$$

Gradient intuition: $\beta$ scales update / KL strength. $\sigma(\hat{r}_l-\hat{r}_w)$ is a per-example weight: big when model ranks loser too highly, small when it already gets pair correct. Push winner up, loser down.

Baselines & Variants

Best-of-$N$: sample $N$ outputs from SFT, score with RM/verifier, pick best; no weight update, but inference-expensive. Preference tuning: SFT on preferred outputs only; simple, ignores negative signal from loser. GRPO: DeepSeek-R1-style variant; compute advantage relative to a group of sampled responses instead of a separate value network.

Alignment Issues

Reward hacking / length bias: RM can overvalue longer answers; older datasets saw large reward gains explained mainly by verbosity (~85% in some analyses). Sycophancy: model agrees with user's stated belief even when false; tends to rise with RL steps/model size. Sandbagging: weaker answers for prompts framing user as less educated. Annotator disagreement: preference labels are noisy and value-laden.

RL with Verifiers: for checkable tasks (math, code), use a verifier instead of a learned reward model to reduce reward hacking. Generate $N$ candidates → verifier scores → pick best. Common in modern post-training pipelines.

KEY DISTINCTIONS

Encoder-only (BERT: bidirectional, classification) vs. Decoder-only (GPT: autoregressive, generation) vs. Enc-Dec (T5: both). Static embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe: one vec per word) vs. Contextualized (ELMo, BERT: context-dependent). Teacher forcing (use ground truth) vs. Scheduled sampling (gradual transition). PPO (4 models, on-policy, advantage-based, complex, more stable for hard tasks) vs. DPO (2 models, off-policy, direct preference loss, simpler, may plateau). REINFORCE (basic policy gradient, high variance, simple) vs. PPO (adds value baseline + KL constraint) vs. DPO (bypasses RM entirely). Greedy (fast, suboptimal) vs. Beam (better, repetitive) vs. Sampling (diverse, risky). Intrinsic (similarity/analogy) vs. Extrinsic (downstream performance) evaluation.
